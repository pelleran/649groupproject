SI 649 Final Report:
Hate Crime Trends in the US Before and After 2014

By: Youngsoo Choy, Weilong Liu, Cameron McLaughlin, Andrea Pellerito, Tasha Torchon

Executive Summary

Our goal is to track trends in hate crime between 2008 and 2018, with the intent of investigating the cause of the increase of hate crimes that began after 2014. We begin with a high-level view of all hate crimes and their bias motivations and drill down to the most common bias motivation (race) and the type of crime (violent vs. nonviolent). We analyze how these phenomena have changed over time and compare our findings with others1,2 who have also analyzed the data.

Comparing the dates of significant political events and the continual uptick in hate crime beginning in 2015, we can surmise that the change in presidential administration may be correlated with hate crime; however, it is not necessarily the root cause of discord. We offer some examples from 2014 onwards to suggest that the recent rise in hate crimes may be an indication of greater societal and political unrest. We also invite our audience to explore the visualizations to draw their own conclusions based upon the data.

In order to communicate the relationship between the political climate and hate crime data, we have designed four different visualizations:

  • a stacked area chart showing the bias motivations of hate crimes by year,
  • a pyramid chart showing the number of victims and offenders of each race over time,
  • a table bubble plot illustrating the relationship between offenders’ race and ethnicity and bias motivation over time, and
  • a bar chart comparing violent crimes vs. nonviolent crimes by year.

We are tracking trends from 2008 (the year President Obama was first elected) through 2018 (the most recent available data) using the FBI’s Hate Crime Statistics datasets.

Insights

Hate Crime by Year

This stacked bar chart tracks the number of hate crimes reported between 2008 and 2018, distinguished by motivation bias. From 2008 to 2014, there is a gradual decrease of all hate crime, reaching its lowest point in 2014. It goes up again the following year (2015) and in 2018 reaches its second highest point during this period at a total of 9,046 (compared to 2008’s 9,160). The image below also points out some noteworthy occurrences from the time period.

The midterm elections of 2014 indicated that Americans had become increasingly disillusioned with politics3, as “only 36.3 percent of voters weigh[ed] in” and “the Republicans took control of the Senate and held on to their firm grip of the House of Representatives”. According to ‘The Biggest News Stories of 2014’,2014 was also the year several instances of police brutality became public, including Michael Brown’s shooting by a police officer in Ferguson, which incited riots and violence and led to an increase in scrutiny on the use of force by police4. Then, on June 16, 2015, Donald Trump officially began his Republican presidential campaign that featured increasingly divisive rhetoric. He was elected president on Tuesday, November 8, 2016, despite having lost the popular vote5, which further highlights the tensions present in the country.

Overall, the hate crimes based on each bias motivation (sexual orientation, religion, etc) did not see significant proportional changes from one year to the next except in the category of racial bias motivation. Between 2008 and 2013, racial bias accounted for 50% of all reported hate crime motivations. It reached its lowest percentage (49%) in 2014. Afterwards, however, this rises to approximately 55% for the period of 2015 to 2018, which is a noticeable change compared to other motivations.

The chart above is interactive. Feel free to hover over or click the different biases to get more details about the numbers in each category between 2008 and 2018. (Note the adjusting scale when drilled down).

Offenders and Victims by Race6

If we look more closely at the largest bias category (race), we can see that with some exceptions, this pattern of high-low-high persists not just in aggregate but for individual races as well. For instance, there were:

  • 4,229 White offenders and 829 White victims in 2008,
  • 2,699 White offenders and 734 White victims in 2014, and
  • 3,359 White offenders and 1,038 White victims in 2018.

This seems to indicate that the unrest or frustration of the time affected the general population, not just certain groups.

Above is a pyramid population chart showing offenders and victims by race. Use the slider to move from one year to the next, and hover over individual bars to see their totals to check if the numbers given confirm the trend.

N.B. Data on Latino offenders were not collected before 2012. Data on Arab people were not collected before 2015.

Offenders’ Race by Racial and Ethnic Bias Motivation

Another way to examine the pervasiveness of the trend is to look at interactions among racial groups. From 2008 to 2013, the total number of offenders with racial/ethnic bias motivations decreased from a high of 5,598 to a low of 3,165. During this time period, there was a significant drop in hate crimes committed by unknown and White offenders, especially Anti-Black or African American bias motivation. After 2013, the total number of racially and ethnically motivated crimes start to increase, with 4,779 crimes in 2018. In particular, from 2013 to 2018, we see:

  • Unknown offenders, Anti-Black or African American bias increase from 175 to 578,
  • White offenders, Anti-Hispanic or Latino bias increase from 220 to 322, and
  • White offenders, Anti-Arab bias (which the FBI began tracking in 2015) increase from 25 to 62.

The table bubble chart above shows the interactions described in the preceding paragraph. Use the slider to get a view of each year, and hover over the bubbles to get specific numbers.

Comparison of Violent and Nonviolent Crimes

Finally, the chart below, comparing violent (i.e. assault or murder) and nonviolent (i.e. graffiti or robbery) hate crimes, follows the same trends present in the other graphs, with an overall decrease in hate crimes from 2008 that hits a low in 2014 before starting to rise again in 2015. However, the proportion of violent to nonviolent crimes increased significantly in 2018 “to a 16-year high” according to NPR, suggesting an increasingly insidious nature to the hate crimes. This is particularly striking when the bars from 2018 are compared to those in 2008, where the number of violent hate crimes is slightly higher in 2018 (5,566) than in 2008 (5,542) despite the total number of hate crimes being slightly lower in 2018 than 2008.

This chart is also interactive. Feel free to hover over the bars for more details.

Conclusion

Our visualizations indicate that the overall trends in hate crime saw a watershed moment in 2014. We cannot conclude with certainty that Trump is the sole or largest cause, especially given other indications of divisiveness in the country that line up with the rise in hate crimes. It seems that both situations (the rise of Trump and in hate crimes) may be the product of the broader socio-political landscape; we defer to political and social scientists to find the precise root causes. One option for a next step would be to analyze and visualize hate crime data over a longer period of time. When paired with other historical information, this could provide greater insight into the nature of hate crimes, how they have changed over time, and whether those changes are predictable.
